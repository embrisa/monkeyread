How to Play Monkeyread

  1. Select your Game Mode and Progression Style.
  2. Click "Start Game". Glyphs (letters) will flash. Pay attention to their order!
  3. The input boxes are color-coded for order: Red (1st), Green (2nd), Blue (3rd). Enter the glyphs you saw into the corresponding colored slots.
  4. Scoring & Speed:
    • Points are awarded based on the current display speed (faster speed = more points).
    • Perfect (All glyphs correct & in order): Max points for the round! Display speed for the next round significantly decreases (gets faster).
    • Correct glyphs, wrong order: Good points! Display speed moderately decreases.
    • Any incorrect glyph / Not all glyphs identified: No points for the round. Display speed slightly increases (gets slower).
  5. Progression: Based on your "Progression Style" selection.
  6. The game continues, adapting the speed to your performance. Aim for precision and speed!

How Fast Can We See?

Did you know? The human brain can identify an image seen for as little as 13 milliseconds—that's less than 1/20th of the time it takes to blink! But the journey from light hitting your eye to conscious recognition is a rapid, multi-stage process.

The Stages of Seeing

  1. Light Hits the Eye: Photoreceptor cells in your retina convert light into electrical signals.
  2. To the Brain: Signals travel via the optic nerve to the brain's relay station (LGN), then to the primary visual cortex (V1), where basic features are processed (about 100 ms).
  3. Making Sense of It: Higher brain areas identify what and where things are—conscious recognition can take 150–200 ms or more.

What Affects How Fast We See?

  • Where You're Looking: Center vision (fovea) is fastest and sharpest; side vision is slower and blurrier.
  • Brightness & Contrast: Brighter, high-contrast images are seen faster.
  • Complexity: Simple shapes are processed faster than complex or cluttered scenes.

Fun Facts

  • Visual "Refresh Rate": Our eyes can distinguish flashes up to about 60 times per second (60 Hz) before it looks continuous—this is called the Flicker Fusion Frequency.
  • Rapid Eye Movements: Your eyes make quick jumps (saccades) 2–3 times per second. During these, vision is briefly suppressed to prevent blur, and your brain stitches together a stable view.

In short: Humans can spot images in just 13 ms, but full recognition takes a few hundred milliseconds. Where you look, how bright or complex something is, and your brain's clever processing all shape your visual speed!
